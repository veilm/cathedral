# LCOE Benchmarks 2025

Levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) benchmarks for 2025 show a notable divergence: solar and wind costs rose slightly after years of decline, while battery storage costs fell sharply. These numbers are the baseline for evaluating any new generation or storage investment.

## 2025 Figures

From BNEF's 2025 data:
- **Fixed-tilt solar PV**: $39/MWh (up 6% from 2024)
- **Onshore wind**: $40/MWh (also rose)
- **Offshore wind**: $100/MWh
- **Battery storage (BESS)**: $78/MWh (down 27% YoY)
- **Combined cycle gas**: $102/MWh
- **Conventional nuclear**: $258/MWh

Lazard's 2025 range for US utility-scale solar is broader: $38-78/MWh, averaging $58/MWh — a 4% YoY decrease by their methodology. The discrepancy with BNEF reflects different assumptions about financing, capacity factors, and geographic mix.

## The Solar Anomaly

The 6% solar LCOE increase broke a 15-year trend of continuous decline (97% total reduction since 2010). BNEF's own 2025 projection had been $35/MWh; actual came in at $39/MWh. BNEF analyst Jenny Chase attributed the miss to three factors: rising capital costs across energy markets broadly, "power price cannibalisation and curtailment" increasing project risk premiums, and elevated long-term Euro swap rates despite ECB rate cuts.

Chase characterized the increase as "anomalous" — a financing-environment artifact, not a technology regression. Module costs continued falling; it's the cost of capital and revenue risk that pushed LCOE up. This matters for investment analysis: the underlying technology learning curve is intact, but project-level returns are more sensitive to interest rates and curtailment risk than they were when solar was expensive enough to always find buyers.

## Battery Divergence

The 27% BESS cost decline stands out against the solar/wind increases. This reflects continued lithium-ion manufacturing scale-up, the LFP chemistry shift (LFP pack prices hit $70/kWh in 2025, down 45% from 2024), and emerging competition from [[sodium-ion-batteries]]. At $78/MWh, grid batteries are now cheaper than combined cycle gas ($102/MWh) for peaking applications. The crossover is recent and significant — see [[iron-air-batteries]] for how long-duration storage economics differ from these short-duration benchmarks.
