# Iron-Air Batteries

Iron-air batteries are an emerging long-duration energy storage technology that uses the reversible oxidation of iron (rusting and un-rusting) to store and release electricity. They target 100-hour storage durations at dramatically lower cost per kWh than lithium-ion, potentially filling the multi-day storage gap that current 2-4 hour batteries cannot address.

## How It Works

Charging uses electricity to reduce iron oxide back to metallic iron. Discharging reverses this — iron reacts with oxygen from the air to form rust, releasing energy. The active materials are iron, air, and water, all abundant and cheap. The low energy density means iron-air is unsuitable for vehicles or portable applications, but for stationary grid storage where size and weight don't matter, the economics are potentially transformative.

## Form Energy

Form Energy is the leading iron-air developer, targeting $20/kWh at manufacturing scale — roughly 5-10x cheaper per kWh than current lithium-ion. Their manufacturing facility is 850,000 sq ft, with plans to reach 1 million sq ft by 2028. Commercial deployments are beginning: a pilot with Great River Energy in Minnesota went online summer 2025, with two 10 MW projects for Xcel Energy and a 15 MW facility for Georgia Power scheduled for 2026. The largest announced project is a 300 MW / 30 GWh deployment through a Google-Xcel Energy agreement (March 2026). Market projections estimate 1-5 GWh total deployments through 2027, scaling to 10-25 GWh/year in 2028-2030.

## Ore Energy

Ore Energy, a Dutch startup (~40 employees, ~$23M in funding), brought the first grid-connected iron-air battery online in the EU in mid-May 2025, at Delft University of Technology in the Netherlands. The pilot is under 1 MWh with up to 100-hour duration, with 6-12 months of testing planned. Their target commercial unit is a 4.2 MWh containerized system. Notably, the system was fully built using European supply chain resources.

## Investment Implications

If Form Energy's $20/kWh target holds at scale, iron-air would make multi-day storage economically viable for the first time. Current [[sodium-ion-batteries]] and LFP lithium-ion compete well at 2-4 hour durations, but their costs scale linearly with duration — a 100-hour lithium-ion system is economically absurd. Iron-air's value is in the durations nothing else can reach cheaply. The risk is execution: manufacturing scale-up, real-world cycle performance, and whether the 2028-2030 volume ramp materializes. The technology is no longer theoretical — grid-connected units exist — but commercial-scale validation is still 2-3 years away.
